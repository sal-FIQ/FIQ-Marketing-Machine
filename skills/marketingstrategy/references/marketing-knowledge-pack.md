# Marketing knowledge pack

Use this file for stronger strategic judgment. It distills durable frameworks, books, and public reference material into reusable planning guidance for revenue-aligned marketing strategy.

## How to use this file

- Use **research-backed principles** for core decisions about positioning, brand building, demand creation, segmentation, and measurement.
- Use **books and practitioner frameworks** to structure the plan and explain choices.
- Do not quote sources at length. Internalize the logic and apply it.
- When two ideas conflict, prefer the one that better fits the company’s sales motion, market maturity, and revenue model.

## 1) Foundational strategy ideas to apply repeatedly

### A. Positioning is the foundation, not a tagline exercise
Use this whenever the company sells a complex offer, is hard to explain, competes against multiple alternatives, or has weak win-rate support from marketing.

Core implication:
- clarify the competitive alternative
- define differentiated value in the context buyers already understand
- choose the market category carefully
- build messaging from positioning, not the other way around

Best supporting book:
- **Obviously Awesome** by April Dunford. The author’s official book page and publisher page position the book as a step-by-step method for finding a product’s unique value, selecting the best market context, and leveraging market trends in positioning. Relevant when the skill needs stronger positioning architecture or clearer differentiated framing.

Related source pages:
- April Dunford books page: https://www.aprildunford.com/books
- Page Two publisher page for *Obviously Awesome*: https://pagetwo.com/book/obviously-awesome/
- Page Two publisher page for *Sales Pitch*: https://pagetwo.com/book/sales-pitch/

### B. Messaging should compress strategic truth into clear buying language
Use this when the company is feature-heavy, internally jargon-heavy, or trying to sell transformation rather than a simple tool.

Core implication:
- write for buyer comprehension, not internal pride
- make the message simple enough for sales, partners, analysts, and candidates to repeat consistently
- connect message to buying situations, not just product functions

Best supporting book:
- **Make It Punchy** by Emma Stratton. Use as a messaging simplification lens when the strategy needs crisp, human, repeatable language instead of abstract claims.

Related source page:
- Page Two publisher page for *Make It Punchy*: https://pagetwo.com/book/make-it-punchy/

### C. Most of the market is not buying right now
Use this whenever the plan risks over-optimizing for short-term lead capture.

Core implication:
- demand capture matters, but it only serves current buyers
- demand creation and brand memory matter because most future revenue comes from buyers who are currently out of market
- build both current pipeline and future preference

Research-backed reference:
- LinkedIn B2B Institute’s 95-5 Rule explains that most buyers are out-market today and argues for balancing lead generation with broad brand reach.
- Ehrenberg-Bass Institute explains the same principle and ties it to long purchase cycles and the limits of immediate-response thinking.

Related source pages:
- LinkedIn B2B Institute 95-5 Rule: https://business.linkedin.com/marketing-solutions/b2b-institute/b2b-research/trends/95-5-rule
- Ehrenberg-Bass 95:5 rule overview: https://marketingscience.info/news-and-insights/the-955-rule-why-b2b-growth-starts-long-before-the-purchase

### D. Memory matters before search
Use this when the company wants stronger brand authority, category salience, or better performance from already-existing demand.

Core implication:
- the goal is not only to harvest intent but to shape recall before intent appears
- messaging should map to buying situations buyers actually encounter
- content should build memory structures, not just drive clicks

Research-backed reference:
- LinkedIn B2B Institute’s work on **Category Entry Points** explains how to connect the brand to the situations that trigger demand.

Related source pages:
- Category Entry Points in a B2B World: https://business.linkedin.com/marketing-solutions/b2b-institute/cep-in-b2b/
- Research library overview: https://business.linkedin.com/marketing-solutions/b2b-institute/b2b-research

### E. Measurement should connect brand and pipeline, not force everything into last-touch logic
Use this whenever the company measures mostly leads, form fills, or vanity metrics.

Core implication:
- separate north-star commercial metrics from operating metrics and diagnostic metrics
- report both immediate pipeline impact and longer-horizon brand indicators
- avoid pretending that all value is attributable in a neat last-touch model

Research-backed reference:
- LinkedIn B2B Institute’s **CMO Scorecard** reframes measurement around creative, media, and outcome principles rather than pure lead volume.

Related source page:
- The CMO Scorecard: https://business.linkedin.com/marketing-solutions/b2b-institute/cmo-scorecard

## 2) Strategy patterns to use in plans

### Pattern: enterprise authority build
Choose this when:
- deal size is high
- buying committees are large
- credibility and trust are essential
- the buyer fears implementation risk or career risk

What the strategy should emphasize:
- narrow ICP and buying committee map
- proof-rich messaging
- strong executive and expert thought leadership
- case studies, benchmarks, research, and partner validation
- account-based motions, not only broad lead gen

### Pattern: hybrid demand creation plus demand capture
Choose this when:
- the market is active but crowded
- some demand exists already, but the company also needs future preference
- leadership expects pipeline now and stronger market authority over time

What the strategy should emphasize:
- broad awareness and thought leadership for out-market buyers
- capture programs for in-market demand
- content mapped to category entry points and buying triggers
- reporting that distinguishes short-term and long-term effects

### Pattern: ABM for high-value, complex sales
Choose this when:
- ACV is high
- the list of target accounts is knowable
- buying committees are messy and multistakeholder
- personalization will materially improve win odds

What the strategy should emphasize:
- target account selection logic
- buyer committee mapping
- personalized content and sales enablement
- tighter marketing and sales orchestration
- account engagement and opportunity creation as core metrics

## 3) Recommended books to keep in mind

Use these books as framing devices when the strategy needs more rigor.

### Positioning and messaging
1. **Obviously Awesome** — April Dunford  
   Best for: positioning, market category choice, differentiation, messaging foundation.

2. **Sales Pitch** — April Dunford  
   Best for: translating positioning into a sales-ready narrative that helps buyers choose confidently.

3. **Make It Punchy** — Emma Stratton  
   Best for: simplifying technical or abstract messaging into clear buyer language.

4. **Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind** — Al Ries and Jack Trout  
   Best for: understanding the classic logic of occupying mental space in a crowded market. Use as a foundational mental model, but modernize it for today’s channels and buying behavior.

### Brand, demand, and growth
5. **How Brands Grow** / **How B2B Brands Grow** themes — Byron Sharp, Ehrenberg-Bass, and LinkedIn B2B Institute related work  
   Best for: mental and physical availability, category buying behavior, and why broad reach still matters in B2B.

6. **The B2B Social Media Book** — Kipp Bodnar and Jeffrey L. Cohen  
   Best for: older but still useful thinking on lead generation through social and content systems. Use selectively for channel ideas, not as the sole strategic backbone.

7. **Designing B2B Brands** — Carlos Martinez Onaindia and Brian Resnick  
   Best for: enterprise brand system thinking and global B2B brand consistency.

Relevant publisher or source pages:
- Wiley on *The B2B Social Media Book*: https://newsroom.wiley.com/press-releases/press-release-details/2012/Having-a-B2B-Marketing-Strategy-Matters/default.aspx

## 4) Practical decision rules

### When positioning is fuzzy
Start with:
- best-fit customer
- competitive alternative
- unique value or unique capability
- proof the market will believe
- best category frame

Then write messaging only after those are clear.

### When leadership wants more leads
Translate the ask into:
- pipeline created
- pipeline influenced
- conversion rate improvement
- speed to opportunity
- strategic account penetration
- demand creation for the 95 percent not yet buying

### When the strategy is drifting into channel overload
Force prioritization by asking:
- which channels match the buying motion?
- which channels can the likely team execute well?
- which channels support both authority and pipeline?
- what should be explicitly deprioritized?

### When attribution becomes the whole story
Rebalance by including:
- commercial outcomes
- funnel performance
- account engagement
- branded search or direct traffic where relevant
- evidence of market authority and sales effectiveness support

## 5) What not to do

- do not treat brand and demand as separate universes
- do not copy consumer-brand advice directly into enterprise B2B without adapting it
- do not create vague messaging pillars with no proof
- do not promise precise funnel numbers without evidence
- do not recommend ABM just because it sounds sophisticated
- do not use books or thought leadership as decoration; convert them into clear strategic choices
