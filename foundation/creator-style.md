## 1. Voice in One Sentence

The atomic unit

An empowering, human voice that treats users as capable builders — conversational and clear without being dumbed down, playful without being flippant, and always permission-giving rather than prescriptive.

## 2. Tone Characteristics

The 5 defining traits

1. Permission-giving, not prescriptive

"Here's what's possible" not "here's how you should work." We show options and trust users to decide. We never impose a methodology. This is the single most important trait — it mirrors the product itself.

2. Conversational, not corporate

We use "you" directly. Contractions are standard. We write like a smart colleague explaining something, not like a brand talking at an audience. No marketing-speak, no jargon walls.

3. Empowering, not patronizing

We assume competence. We don't explain obvious things or over-qualify statements. The reader is a capable person who wants better tools, not a student who needs hand-holding.

4. Playful, not silly

We have personality — wit, warmth, occasional humor. But we never sacrifice clarity for a joke. The playfulness shows up in word choice and rhythm, not in forced quips.

5. Direct, not verbose

Say it in fewer words. Every sentence earns its place. If a paragraph can be a sentence, make it a sentence. Notion's product is about reducing clutter — the writing should feel the same way.

## 3. Writing Patterns

Structure & rhythm

Sentence structure:

Short sentences lead. Longer sentences follow to explain. Fragments are welcome when they add punch. The rhythm alternates — staccato then flow, staccato then flow. Never more than 3 sentences in a paragraph on social. Blog can go to 4-5 but keeps paragraphs visually light.

Formatting habits:

* Headlines are clear and benefit-driven, not clever for clever's sake
* Heavy use of subheadings for scannability — readers skim first, read second
* Bullet points for lists of 3+ items — never buried in a paragraph
* Bold for key terms and concepts on first mention
* Screenshots and visuals inline with text, not separated into galleries
* White space is a feature, not wasted space

Opening pattern:

Lead with what the user can do, not what we built. Best openings start with a benefit or a tension the reader recognizes. "Your workspace should adapt to how you work, not the other way around." Not: "We're excited to announce a new feature."

Closing pattern:

End with a clear next action — try it, duplicate the template, explore the feature. No "in conclusion" summaries. The closing should make the reader want to open Notion immediately.

## 4. Always Do / Never Do

Non-negotiable rules

### Always

* Lead with the user's outcome, not our feature
* Show real workspaces and real use cases — never stock setups
* Acknowledge that power can be intimidating — then make it approachable
* Use "you" and "your" — this is about them, not us
* Include something actionable — a template, a setup, a feature to try
* Show multiple use cases for the same feature — reinforce flexibility

### Never

* Enterprise jargon — "streamline workflows," "drive alignment," "unlock productivity"
* Be prescriptive — "the best way to use Notion is..." violates the core brand promise
* Announce features without showing what they enable
* Talk down to users or over-explain basics
* Use fear or scarcity tactics
* Sound interchangeable — if you can swap "Notion" for "Asana" and the copy works, it's wrong

## 5. Sounds Like Us vs. Doesn't Sound Like Us

Calibration examples

| "Your workspace should adapt to how you work, not the other way around." | "Our platform provides a streamlined solution for team collaboration." |
| "Build your own CRM. Or a wiki. Or a project tracker. Or all three." | "Notion offers comprehensive project management capabilities." |
| "One workspace. Less tab chaos." | "Consolidate your technology stack with our all-in-one solution." |
| "Here's a template. Duplicate it. Make it yours." | "Download our preconfigured workflow to optimize your processes." |
