## 1. Discovery

How they find us

**YouTube** — Setup tours and productivity videos. Biggest discovery channel. Creators like Thomas Frank, Ali Abdaal, and hundreds of smaller channels. ~45% of new users mention YouTube.

**Peer recommendation** — "Hey, you'd love Notion." Word of mouth is massive. Notion is a product people talk about unprompted.

**TikTok / Instagram** — Aesthetic workspace content. #NotionTok and study setup communities drive younger user awareness.

**Reddit** — r/Notion (750k+ members), r/productivity, r/MacSetups. Tool comparison threads are a key discovery vector.

**Product Hunt** — Templates, integrations, and extensions get featured regularly. Drives builder/early adopter awareness.

**Google Search** — "best productivity tool," "Confluence alternative," "all-in-one workspace." Notion ranks for comparison and category terms.

What triggers first awareness:

Two primary paths: (1) They *see* someone else's Notion workspace — a colleague's screen, a YouTube video, a Reddit post — and think "I want my setup to look like that." Discovery is often visual, not problem-driven. (2) They hit a tool sprawl breaking point and actively search for consolidation. The emotional driver behind these moments lives in `audience-delight.md`.

## 2. Evaluation

What they ask, compare, and object to

Questions they ask during evaluation:

* "Can it actually replace [Asana / Confluence / Google Docs / Trello] for my team?"
* "Is it hard to learn? How long before I'm productive?"
* "Will my team actually adopt it, or will it just be me?"
* "What happens to my data if I want to leave? Am I locked in?"
* "Is it secure enough for our company? SOC 2? SSO?"
* "How does it compare to [specific competitor] for [specific use case]?"

Comparisons they make:

**Notion vs. Confluence** — Most common for teams with Atlassian stack. Side-by-side comparison. Notion's UX wins visually; Confluence's Jira integration wins for dev teams.

**Notion vs. Asana** — When primary need is project management. Asana wins for strict PM methodology. Notion wins when they also need docs/wiki alongside projects.

**Notion vs. Google Docs + Sheets** — "Free and good enough" comparison. Notion wins on organization and databases. Google wins on real-time collaboration and familiarity.

**Notion vs. Obsidian** — Individual users choosing between cloud collaboration and local-first privacy. Different philosophies, different users.

Objections that surface:

"It looks complicated — my team won't use it." *(Most common. Blank canvas intimidates non-power-users.)*

"We already have [tool] and switching costs are high." *(Data migration fear.)*

"It's not a real project management tool." *(Teams with complex dependencies push back.)*

"What about offline access?" *(Remote workers and travelers.)*

"Is the paid tier worth it for my small team?" *(Price sensitivity on per-seat costs.)*

## 3. Conversion Triggers

What closes the deal

**The "aha" workspace moment** — They build their first real workspace and see databases, linked views, and templates come together. Product sells itself once they experience the flexibility. Time to aha: 2-5 hours of real use.

**A colleague already using it** — If one person on the team is in Notion, adoption friction drops. Internal champions drive most team conversions.

**Template that matches their use case** — Duplicating a template and seeing it work immediately is the fastest convert path.

**Storage or guest limits hit** — Free tier limits create natural upgrade triggers when usage gets serious.

Proof points that close (ranked):

1

**Seeing a real workspace in action** — a demo, colleague's setup, or YouTube walkthrough matching their use case

2

**The consolidation math** — "We pay $X/mo for Asana + Confluence + Google Workspace. Notion Business is $25/seat and replaces all three."

3

**Template library breadth** — "There's already a template for exactly what I need" removes the blank canvas objection

4

**Community size** — 750k on r/Notion, thousands of YouTube tutorials signals staying power and support

5

**Named company usage** — "If [company they respect] uses it, it's credible for our stage"

## 4. Stall & Churn Points

Where they get stuck or leave

**Blank canvas paralysis**

Sign up, see empty workspace, don't know where to start. Too many options, no guidance. #1 drop-off point for new users.

**Template overload**

Browse 10,000 templates, can't decide, postpone setup indefinitely. Analysis paralysis masked as research.

**Integration gap**

Need Notion to connect to a specific tool. Integration doesn't exist or is clunky. Keep parallel system, drift back to it.

**Team onboarding**

Champion builds workspace. Getting the team to use it requires training, habit change, and buy-in. Many teams stall here.

Why they leave:

**Team didn't adopt** — #1 churn reason. Individual enthusiasm doesn't survive team inertia. If team doesn't use it within 30 days, it dies.

**Found a specialized tool** — Deep PM features (Asana) or deep documentation (Confluence) won over flexibility.

**Performance at scale** — Large workspaces with thousands of pages get slow. Power users hit performance ceilings.

**Context change** — Graduated, switched jobs, freelancer joined a company with existing tools. Use was tied to a specific life context.

## 5. Expansion

Upgrades & referrals

Free → Plus

Individual power user hits storage limits or wants unlimited blocks. Invested enough time that $10/month feels obvious to protect it.

Plus → Business

Team use case emerges. Need guest access, shared workspaces, advanced permissions. Triggered by a second or third person wanting in.

Business → Enterprise

Security requirements: SSO, SCIM, audit logs. Triggered by IT or compliance review, not end user. Procurement-driven.

What triggers referrals:

The "you should try this" moment happens when someone *sees* the champion's workspace: "that looks amazing — how did you build that?" Referrals are almost always visual — triggered by a clean, functional workspace on someone's screen. Product aesthetics are the single biggest referral driver. Second trigger: someone complains about tool sprawl and the Notion user says "I used to have that problem."
