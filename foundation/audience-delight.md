# Audience Delight Profile

**Last updated:** 2026-04-01

**Author:** [Marketing Team]

This is NOT a traditional ICP. No firmographics. No "decision-maker at a 200-500 person SaaS company."

This file captures what makes your audience light up. What they share. What they complain about. What language they use when no one from your company is listening. What moves them from passively scrolling to actively engaging.

**Which skills use this file:** Any skill producing audience-facing content — posts, ad copy, emails, landing pages, lead magnets.

## 1. Who They Are (In Their Own Words)

Identity — not demographics

How they describe themselves:

"I'm organized but overwhelmed." They're knowledge workers, product managers, designers, founders, and team leads who are drowning in tool sprawl — Slack plus Asana plus Google Drive plus a notes app — and looking for one place that does it all. They describe themselves as "systems thinkers" who want to build the perfect workspace, not just use someone else's. At the individual level, they're building a "second brain." At the team level, they want a "single source of truth."

How they want to be seen by their peers:

The person who has their shit together. The one whose workspace is both beautiful and functional — who can pull up any document, any project, any note instantly. They want to be known for having a system, not just being busy. In team settings, they want to be the person who brought the tool that actually stuck.

Sophistication level: `Intermediate to Advanced`

They know what relational databases are, they've tried multiple productivity tools, and they have opinions about Kanban vs. list view. Don't explain what a template is. Do show them what's possible with a template they haven't seen yet.

## 2. What Makes Them Light Up

Delight signals

Topics they can't stop talking about:

* "My Notion setup" — they share screenshots of their workspaces the way people share home renovations. The before/after of going from chaos to a clean dashboard is genuinely exciting to them.
* Templates that save real time — not generic ones, but opinionated templates built for a specific workflow that they can steal and adapt.
* The feeling of replacing 3 tools with 1 — every time they kill a subscription or close a tab permanently, it's a small victory.
* New Notion features that unlock something they couldn't do before — especially AI features that work inside their existing workspace.

The "finally someone said it" moments:

* "Most productivity tools force you into someone else's workflow. I just want a blank canvas that I can make mine."
* "I don't need another project management tool. I need one workspace that handles docs, projects, and notes without making me context-switch."
* "Confluence is where documentation goes to die."
* "The best productivity system is the one you actually use, not the one with the most features."

Content they forward to colleagues:

Notion setup tours with real screenshots. "Here's how our team actually uses Notion for X" posts — not marketing case studies, but real practitioners showing their actual workspace. Templates they can duplicate with one click. Quick video walkthroughs (under 5 minutes) showing a specific workflow.

What makes them reach out:

Two trigger moments: (1) They just spent 20 minutes searching across 4 different tools for one document and hit their breaking point. (2) Their team is growing and the informal systems (Slack threads, Google Docs scattered in folders) are breaking down. They need a single place before things get worse.

## 3. What Frustrates Them

Pain points — in their language, not yours

1. Tool sprawl is killing productivity

How they describe it:

"I have 12 tabs open across 5 different apps just to manage one project." / "We pay for Asana, Confluence, Google Docs, and Slack and none of them talk to each other."

Why it's still unresolved:

Each tool does one thing well enough that teams adopt it, but no one owns the "consolidation" decision. Switching costs feel high because data is spread everywhere.

2. Setup paralysis — the blank canvas problem

How they describe it:

"I spent 3 hours building my Notion setup instead of doing actual work." / "There are 10,000 templates and I don't know which one to use." / "I know Notion can do everything but I can't figure out where to start."

Why it's still unresolved:

Notion's flexibility is both its strength and its trap. The more powerful the tool, the more decisions the user has to make before they get value.

3. Team adoption failure

How they describe it:

"I built the perfect Notion workspace. Nobody on my team uses it." / "I'm the only one who updates the wiki."

Why it's still unresolved:

Individual enthusiasm doesn't transfer automatically to teams. Most Notion content focuses on personal setup, not on how to get a team of 20 to actually adopt it.

Objections and misconceptions they hold:

* "If I just find the perfect template, my productivity problems will be solved." *(The template is 10% — the habit of using it is 90%.)*
* "Notion is too complex for my team." *(It's as complex as you make it. The best team setups are intentionally simple.)*
* "We need a dedicated project management tool — Notion can't handle real project management." *(It can, but it requires intentional setup, not just dumping tasks in.)*

## 4. Their Vocabulary

Language that signals insider vs. outsider

| Concept | They say | They DON'T say |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Their workspace | "my Notion," "my setup," "my system" | "the platform," "the solution" |
| Organization | "second brain," "single source of truth," "wiki" | "knowledge management system," "documentation repository" |
| Flexibility | "blank canvas," "build what you need," "customizable" | "configurable enterprise solution" |
| Databases | "relational databases," "linked databases," "views" | "data tables," "spreadsheet functionality" |
| Other tools | "tool sprawl," "context switching," "tab hell" | "technology stack consolidation" |
| Templates | "duplicate," "steal this template," "my setup" | "preconfigured workflow solutions" |

In-group terms they use without explanation:

Kanban, relational database, linked view, toggle, callout block, synced block, rollup, formula, API, workspace, teamspace, wiki, embed

Terms that signal "vendor speak" (avoid these):

"enterprise-grade solution," "streamline your workflows," "drive alignment," "unlock productivity," "seamless collaboration," "end-to-end platform," "digital transformation"

## 5. What Pulls Them In vs. Pushes Them Away

Content triggers

### Pulls them in

* Real workspace screenshots — "here's my actual setup, not a demo account"
* Specific use cases with before/after — "how I replaced Trello + Google Docs with one Notion database"
* Templates they can duplicate instantly — zero setup friction
* Quick wins — "set this up in 10 minutes and save 2 hours a week"
* Community-created content — user-to-user carries more trust than brand-to-user
* New features that solve a real pain point they've complained about

### Pushes them away

* Generic productivity advice — "plan your week on Sunday" without showing how
* Enterprise marketing language — "drive cross-functional alignment with our workspace solution"
* Overly complex setups that feel like showing off — if it takes a PhD to understand, it's not helpful
* Ignoring known limitations — pretending performance issues don't exist erodes trust
* Content that treats Notion as just a task manager — their relationship with the tool is much deeper
